The Ho System is a discipline for building things with an agent in the room. Work is cut into hos — bounded sessions, each answering one question and then closing. A closed ho stays closed; when what the project knows changes, a new ho responds. This page is a coherence check: every frozen element of the site, assembled once, doing its real job — before any build code exists.

Read this the way the site will read: the paper is warm, the ink is economical, and the marks that move are the marks that mean something. Nothing here is decorative. Where a thing is gold, a piece has closed.

The five documents of the Kamae chain descend from opinion to session. The koma — the walking piece — appears only where work is steppable. Its promotion is the site's one real animation, and it fires only on real news: a record you are seeing close for the first time.

The step

All site motion is one unit: a 160 ms committed step — 8 px rise, opacity 0→1, cubic-bezier(.2,0,0,1), arriving glyphs seated on the line. A five-node sequence populates on the constant 380 ms beat (build = 1680 ms). The beat is constant on purpose: equal commitments, no momentum, no drift.

What this ho does

This session freezes how a koma state mark seats in the ledger and how a supersession opens. The header carries the only links that auto-render; prose mentions stay plain unless the author linked them.

Execute: 1. render frontmatter as a typed ledger (9rem key column, mono, keys @0.70) 2. seat a 12 px solid koma 0.5 rem before the status value, inked per the state map 3. wire builds-on / supersedes as the header's only live links 4. activating a superseded marker opens facing panes inside the header box

The doing tier speaks in mono; that block above is procedural, and its left rule is sumi, not bero. Bero is earned by register — the aside below, not the instruction above.

Reflect: the ledger tempted us toward a second accent for keys — a quieter grey that wasn't in the ink budget. We held to opacity instead. A key at sumi@0.70 is the same ink as its value, only receded. The restraint is the point: recession is the state map's own grammar.

A closed ho like this one carries a gold koma. Closure is permanent; a superseded record recedes to gold@0.40 rather than vanishing. Forward-only: the record of what was believed stays legible.

5 · Do the koma faces need a subset CJK webfont?

The faces below render in the frozen system stack (Hiragino Mincho ProN / Yu Mincho / Noto Serif JP / serif) — no webfont downloaded:

歩 と

Risk: the glyph you see is whichever mincho the visitor's OS ships. macOS renders Hiragino; Windows without Yu Mincho or Noto falls to a heavier gothic or a tofu box; Linux depends on installed CJK fonts. 歩 and と will shift weight, width, and stroke contrast across platforms — the koma's silhouette is fixed (it's the keyline), but the face is not cross-platform stable. Recommend: subset a single mincho (歩 + と, two glyphs, < 3 KB woff2) and prepend it to the CJK stack — buys consistency at the cost of one small download, and only for the piece. Decision deferred; naming the risk here as asked.

Frictions found

Genuine tensions surfaced while assembling — reported, not resolved, and no frozen value touched.

  1. §5 supersession box vs. §7 card z-order. The supersession reveal (§5) is an in-flow bordered extension of the header; the definition card (§7) is the page's only elevated layer (z-index 10). If a marked term sits inside an open supersession pane, the card correctly floats above — but the pane's gold rules and the card's washi ground are then adjacent at two different depths. Nothing breaks and no value conflicts; it's just the one place two "surfaces" stack. Worth a glance in the spike.
  2. Koma face baseline vs. keyline centering. §1 centers the face at (0.370H, 0.610H) with dominant-baseline: central; the actual optical center of 歩 vs と differs slightly between mincho fonts (deferred item 5), so on some platforms と sits a hair high inside the keyline. Same metrics are honored as frozen; the visible drift is a font-metrics fact, not a spec conflict — it rides along with the CJK-subset decision.
  3. Trail door count vs. nav. §8 freezes exactly six doors (Framework · Practice · Skills · Writing · Walk · Colophon) as placeholders "names bind at build." The nav and the trail both hard-code six; if the real content map yields five or seven doors, the even-spacing geometry and the "first/last inset 8 px" rule still hold, but the placeholder count is load-bearing in two places. Noting it so the build binds names in one source, not two.

No two frozen values contradict. The three above are seams to watch, not conflicts to fix.
