hosystem — design session 2 · palette

Exact values for the three inks + paper, and the state semantics.

Prose samples: plain system serif. Values: system mono. Typefaces are session 3's question.

States apply to hos and records: open = declared, not yet worked · current = the step underway · closed = landed · superseded = closed, later overtaken by a newer ho (forward-only: it stays closed).

All contrast figures computed (sRGB, WCAG 2.x). Body pairs must pass AA (4.5:1). Gold is a mark, never body text; it is held to the 3:1 non-text minimum instead.

atmarcus.net (must be distinct from) the four washi candidates (A–D): cooler, greyer, or whiter — never cream; and gold, never orange

A ho is one bounded step: it opens against a plan, holds one session of work, and closes with a commit. Closed hos stay closed. The thinking that shaped the step lives apart from the step itself — the argument, the rejected alternatives, the reason the decision went the way it did — and the record links into that depth instead of inlining it. Recommendation — A (Aizuri)

  1. The two-register system lives or dies at body size. Sumi is the doing tier, bero the thinking tier; the reader must see the register change in running text. A's aizuri blue (5.8:1) is unmistakably blue at 16 px and still passes AA. B's Great Wave blue (11.0:1) is the more beautiful swatch, but at body size it collapses toward a second black — the register shift disappears exactly where it matters most. Compare the two prose paragraphs before anything else.
  2. The distinction constraint is carried by the paper itself. A's washi is the coolest of the four — a green-grey cast that no one will read as atmarcus cream — so the brand separation doesn't depend only on gold-instead-of-orange. It also makes gold the sole warm note on the page, which protects gold's state semantics: warmth itself becomes a signal of closure.
  3. Flat mineral gold honors "flat fields." B's two-value metal is handsome, but it is a gradient smuggled in as a pair, and it doubles gold's presence when gold should be rare.
  4. A's state map matches forward-only exactly. Closure is permanent; recession is opacity — a superseded ho keeps its gold and simply recedes. C's map revokes gold on supersession, which contradicts the framework (superseding never un-lands anything). D's tarnish is defensible and kept as a parked idea, but it adds a fifth named flat for one rare state.
  5. A finding from D: the whiter the washi, the deeper gold must sit to reach 3:1 — bright paper dulls the metal. A's washi lets gold stay bright enough to read as metal while making the mark minimum. This is another reason the paper should not be near-white.
