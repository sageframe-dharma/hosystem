The pentagon question, argued

Are the chain's nodes the koma, or is the piece reserved for hos?

The case for pieces-as-nodes is unity: five koma descending would be the strongest possible brand mark, trivially legible at glyph scale, and the chain would read as "five steps" at a glance.

But the framework says the chain is not five steps. Kamae (構え) is a stance — the four thinking documents stand; they frame work without being work. Ho (歩) is the step. §1 froze the piece with that meaning attached: the face is 歩 or と, the gold face is a promoted piece, the solid/keyline switch is its inking. A Seed rendered as a koma claims the Seed can step, close, and promote the way a ho does — false. And variant B below shows the mechanical cost: to make five distinguishable koma you must break three frozen values at once (bero strokes where §1 froze sumi, numeral faces where §1 froze 歩/と, bero solid fills where §1 froze sumi/gold). The deviation is visible in the render.

Resolution carried into A, C, D: the piece is reserved for units of bounded, committable work — the ho and its agent-task children. In the chain it appears exactly where work becomes steppable: once at Kamae 5, again in the decomposition row (where §1's sub-24px solid inking does the work unmodified). The chain reads: four frames, then a piece.

Recommendation — Variant A

1. The koma stays what §1 made it. Kamae are stances; the ho is the step. A reserves the piece for bounded work — keyline at the ho node, solid at the agent tasks — so §1's two inkings do real work unmodified, and the diagram teaches the framework's central distinction (frame vs step) by shape alone. B needs three violations of frozen values just to exist; A needs zero.

2. Only A survives all three scales without strain. Its glyph (four bero bars + one solid koma) is a legible, near-square 24 px mark that is recognizably the same drawing as the full diagram. C's glyph is a wide strip a nav corner can't hold; B's koma collapse to dots; D's glyph is a generic bulleted list. At header scale A's piece marker flips to solid form automatically — the reduction rule is §1's own threshold, not a new invention.

Secondary: the rectangle-and-mono-label node is the ASCII box refined — square corners, 1.5 px keyline, flat washi fill — where C and D, whatever their charm, read as typography first and diagram second; and A's node-5 rectangle can physically straddle the tier rule, which is the most honest rendering of "Kamae 5 crosses into the doing register" any variant achieves.

Worth stealing from the losers if wanted later: nothing structural. C's parallel rail and D's gutter both externalize the gradient labels, and both lose the one-node-one-fact locality that A's inline labels keep.
