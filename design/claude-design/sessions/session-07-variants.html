One interaction model (all four variants)

  • Hover / focus — transient reveal. Leaving the term (or blurring) dismisses, unless pinned. The reveal itself is hoverable, so the link is reachable.
  • Tap / click / Enter / Space — pins the reveal. It now survives pointer-leave and blur.
  • Dismiss — second tap on the term, Escape (focus returns to the term), tap/click anywhere outside, or opening another term (in one-at-a-time variants).
  • No navigation — the reader never moves. “full entry →” is the only way out, and it is a link, not the touch.
  • Met (decided below, demoed live in every variant): a term counts as met once a pinned reveal is dismissed — a deliberate touch, not a hover-past. Its dotted underline recedes from bero @0.70 to sumi @0.40.

Recommendation — A

A, the floating card, one at a time.

  • The prose never moves. “The reader stays exactly where they are” is the session’s governing sentence, and A is the only variant that honors it literally in both dimensions: no navigation and no reflow. B shoves the rest of the paragraph down mid-sentence; D moves the reader’s eye to the paragraph foot and back. A puts the plain cut at the point of touch and takes it away without leaving a mark.
  • One geometry, every mode, every width. The same card works identically for hover, tap, and focus, from phone to desktop. C is the most beautiful variant on a wide screen and simply does not exist below ~51rem — it forces a second mechanism on touch devices, which violates “one coherent model” by itself.
  • One-at-a-time is the quiet setting. Three marked terms in one sentence can each be touched, but only one card ever exists. The page can never accumulate open interface. Allow-multiple (C) reads as annotation clutter after two touches.
  • The card is the page’s only floating element, ever — a single, bounded exception to flatness, held to a 1px sumi rule with no shadow and no radius, so it reads as a slip of the same paper laid on top, not as software chrome.

Terms-you’ve-met — decide here

KEEP, in its quietest possible form. Judged against quiet-in-dense-passages: the met state makes dense passages quieter over time, not louder — a met term’s dotted underline recedes from bero @0.70 to sumi @0.40, which is recession by opacity, exactly the grammar the state map already uses for “superseded” and “open.” No gold (met is reader state, not framework state — gold stays untouched), no new marks, no accumulation of emphasis: reading the site literally calms the page down. A term is met only when a pinned reveal is dismissed — a deliberate touch, so hovering across a paragraph marks nothing. The accumulated list is stored client-side with the trail and surfaces wherever the trail renders — that rendering is session 8’s question, not this one. (Try it above: pin a definition, dismiss it, watch the underline recede.)
