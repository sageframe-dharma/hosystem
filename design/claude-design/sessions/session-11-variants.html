the narrow trail — session 11

One question: how the trail renders when the column is narrower than ~480 px. §8’s reading-width geometry is frozen and untouched; this page designs only below it. The coherence check rejected the first narrow rendering on three counts — the thread sat too tight to the door squares, edge lines crossed the met dots, and the captions collided. Every variant below fixes all three. They differ in how.

A — the candidate, given air

Commits to §8’s own grammar, thinned: only doors entered this walk keep their names; the thread drops 20 px clear of the label row; edges anchor bottom→top so no edge can travel along a met-dot row; the two captions merge into one line.

  • clearance — band top = door bottom + 36 px (16 px label slot + 20 px air; §8 was labels + 14) · labels mono 9 (from 10), this-walk doors only
  • crossing — edges leave a node’s bottom-center and land on the next node’s top-center: the dot row at every node’s y is geometrically unreachable · first dot 6 px off the node edge
  • captions — one left line, “your walk · n steps”; the right caption does not render below 480

B — the quiet strip

Commits to no resting labels at all — every door names itself only on touch; with the label row gone the strip is the shortest of the four, and orthogonal edges keep every run between rows, off the dot lines.

  • clearance — band top = door bottom + 18 px, with nothing between the squares and the thread
  • crossing — edges route down · across · down (lane at mid-pitch, head 4 × 4): no segment ever travels at a node’s y · pitch 14
  • captions — “n steps” only, right; “your walk” is spoken by expansion, not at rest

C — the abbreviated row

Commits to keeping all six doors named, at two characters each — and moves the met dots off the thread entirely, into a right-edge ledger gutter no edge can reach.

  • clearance — band top = door bottom + 31 px (labels mono 9 + 16 px air)
  • crossing — met dots live in a 24 px right gutter beyond the last door column; edges span door columns only and cannot reach them
  • captions — stacked left (“your walk” over “n steps”); the right side is empty

D — only the present is named

Commits to the most air and the least ink: 16 px pitch, only the current door labeled, met terms as a count, a single “your walk” caption.

  • clearance — band top = door bottom + 36 px; pitch 16 (the tallest, airiest thread)
  • crossing — bottom→top edge anchoring (as A), and the dots become a “·n” count that sits where they sat
  • captions — “your walk” alone, left; the step count moves to expansion

Recommendation — A

1. Continuity. At the breakpoint the trail must read as the same object, and A is the only variant where 479 px reads as 480 with the numbers moved: same diagonal thread, same full-name labels, same met-dot grammar, same caption content. B changes the thread’s geometric language (orthogonal runs), C changes both the naming and the dot grammar, D deletes the step count — half of what the caption says.

2. The crossing fix is structural, not tuned. Anchoring edges bottom-center → top-center makes an edge on a met-dot row geometrically impossible for any walk at any n — not just the replayed visit. The other two fixes are single numbers (+20 px of air, one merged caption), so the frozen surface is as small as it can be. And the label rule is the coherence check’s own candidate, which §8’s rejection note says stands: fewer names is also exactly what clears the row at 360 px.

3. It keeps the resting form self-carrying. Plan above (six squares, the plan line), walk beneath (the thread), the walked doors named — one look still says plan-vs-walk without a single touch. B asks the reader to touch before anything is named; C’s two-letter doors are terse but not clear, which is the wrong half of the voice rule.

If 360 px still feels crowded at judgment, B is the principled retreat — quietest and shortest — at the cost of an unnamed resting row.
